Gradient Descent, Intuitively

Walking downhill in a fog — the engine under nearly all of machine learning, from linear regression to GPT. Why the simplest optimization idea turns out to be unreasonably powerful, and what makes it fail.

You're standing on a hillside in dense fog. You can't see the valley floor. But you can feel the slope under your feet — which way is down, and how steep. You take a step downhill. At your new position, you feel the slope again and adjust direction. Repeat. Eventually, if the valley isn't too weird, you reach the bottom.

This is gradient descent. The hill is the loss function — a surface that measures how wrong your model is. Every point on the surface corresponds to a particular setting of the model's parameters. The lowest point is the setting that makes the model least wrong. You can't see the whole surface (too many dimensions), but at any point you can compute the local slope — the gradient — and take a step downhill.

From intuition to algorithm

A model has parameters — weights, biases, coefficients — that determine its predictions. For a given set of parameters, the loss function L(θ) computes a single number: how far the predictions are from the truth. Training means finding the θ that minimizes L(θ).

The gradient ∇L(θ) is the vector of partial derivatives of L with respect to each parameter. It points in the direction of steepest ascent. To go downhill, you step in the opposite direction:

vanilla gradient descent
θ_new = θ_old − η · ∇L(θ_old)

The learning rate η (eta) controls step size. Too large and you overshoot the valley, bouncing across the surface without converging. Too small and you reach the bottom sometime next century. Picking η is the first practical problem of optimization, and most of the cleverness in modern optimizers is about adapting it automatically.

The gradient tells you which way is downhill. The learning rate tells you how big a step to take. Every optimizer ever invented is just a variation on how you pick these two things.

Stochastic gradient descent: sampling your way to speed

Computing ∇L(θ) over the entire training set (batch gradient descent) is O(dataset size) per step. For ImageNet (1.2 million images), one gradient computation is expensive. For internet-scale data, it's impossible.

Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) estimates the gradient from a single randomly-chosen example per step. It's noisy — each step is a rough approximation of the true gradient — but it's so cheap you can take thousands of steps in the time one batch step would take. The noise actually helps: it lets SGD escape shallow local minima and saddle points that would trap batch gradient descent.

Mini-batch SGD is the practical compromise: estimate the gradient from a small batch (32–256 examples). It's more stable than single-example SGD and more efficient than full-batch. Every deep learning framework uses it by default. The batch size is a hyperparameter that trades gradient accuracy for computational efficiency — and bigger batches (with learning-rate scaling) are how you train on thousands of GPUs.

Momentum: a ball rolling downhill instead of a person walking

Vanilla SGD takes each step independently, based only on the current gradient. This is inefficient in two cases: when the surface is a long, shallow ravine (SGD zig-zags across the valley instead of following it down), and when the gradient is noisy (SGD oscillates around the minimum without settling).

Momentum adds a velocity term — a running average of past gradients — so each step is influenced by previous steps:

SGD with momentum
v = β·v + η·∇L(θ)
θ = θ − v

β (typically ~0.9) controls how much past gradients persist. In the ravine, momentum smooths out the zig-zags: past steps along the ravine reinforce each other; past steps across cancel out. The optimizer accelerates downhill and decelerates through noise. It reaches the minimum faster and oscillates less once there.

Adam: the default for a reason

Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) combines momentum with adaptive learning rates — each parameter gets its own effective step size, adjusted based on the history of gradients for that specific parameter. Parameters that consistently get large gradients get smaller steps (they're near a steep cliff); parameters that get small, noisy gradients get larger steps (they're on a plateau).

Adam maintains two running averages: the first moment (mean of past gradients — like momentum) and the second moment (mean of squared gradients — a measure of gradient magnitude). The step size for each parameter scales inversely with the square root of the second moment. The result: Adam handles ill-conditioned problems that make vanilla SGD stall, and it's relatively insensitive to the choice of initial learning rate.

Adam is the default optimizer for most deep learning work — transformers, CNNs, diffusion models. Variants (AdamW, which decouples weight decay from the adaptive step; LAMB, for very large batch sizes) tune specific behaviors but keep the adaptive core.

What gradient descent can't do (and what you do about it)

Local minima. The loss surface has valleys that aren't the deepest valley. Vanilla gradient descent stops at the first one it finds. In high dimensions, however, true local minima are rarer than intuition suggests — most critical points in high-dimensional loss surfaces are saddle points (flat in some directions, curved in others), and SGD's noise plus momentum reliably escapes them.

Vanishing and exploding gradients. In deep networks, gradients shrink exponentially as they're backpropagated through many layers (vanishing) or grow exponentially (exploding). Vanishing gradients stall training in early layers; exploding gradients make parameters diverge to infinity. Residual connections (skip layers), careful initialization (He, Xavier), and gradient clipping are the defenses.

Plateaus. Flat regions where the gradient is near zero. The optimizer can stall for thousands of steps with no apparent progress. Adaptive methods (Adam) help by increasing step sizes in flat directions, but sometimes the answer is patience — the optimizer may be working its way across a saddle point that will eventually curve downward.

predict You set the learning rate to 0.1. The loss goes down for a few steps, then shoots up to infinity. You set it to 0.00001. The loss crawls down so slowly you can't tell if it's working. What's happening geometrically, and how does Adam mostly solve this without you having to pick the magic number?

At η=0.1, the step is too large: you're stepping over the valley and landing higher on the opposite slope, each step amplifying the error. At η=0.00001, you're taking microscopic steps that don't reach the bottom in reasonable time. Adam solves this by normalizing step sizes per-parameter: each parameter's effective learning rate is roughly η / sqrt(mean of squared past gradients). Parameters in steep regions automatically get small steps; parameters in flat regions get large steps. The global η can be set to a reasonable default (0.001) and Adam adjusts per-parameter.

when not to Gradient descent requires the loss function to be differentiable. If your model has discrete operations (sampling from a categorical distribution, a threshold, a lookup into a table), the gradient is undefined across those operations. You need either a differentiable relaxation (the Gumbel-softmax trick), a score-function estimator (REINFORCE), or a different optimization family entirely (evolution strategies, Bayesian optimization). This is why training discrete-choice models is fundamentally harder than training continuous ones.
