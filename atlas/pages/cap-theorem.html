The CAP Theorem (and what it actually says)

The most-cited, most-misquoted result in distributed systems. A proof from 2000 about what a replicated store can and cannot guarantee during a partition — and why "pick two out of three" isn't it.

Somewhere on a whiteboard in 2010, someone drew a triangle with C, A, and P at the corners, wrote "pick two" underneath, and launched a decade of architectural arguments founded on a misunderstanding. The real theorem is more precise, more useful, and more interesting.

Eric Brewer presented the idea at PODC 2000; Seth Gilbert and Nancy Lynch proved it formally in 2002. The theorem says: any distributed shared-data system can provide at most two of Consistency, Availability, and Partition tolerance simultaneously.

The "pick two" slogan treats C, A, and P as equals — three knobs you balance. The theorem actually says something different: P isn't a knob. Partitions happen whether you pick them or not. The only real choice is: during a partition, do you favor consistency or availability?

What the three words actually mean

This is where most arguments go wrong, because the formal definitions are specific and not what the English words suggest.

Consistency (in CAP, specifically) means atomic consistency — also called linearizability. Every read sees the most recent write, as if there were only one copy of the data. If client A writes x=5 and acknowledges, and then client B reads x, client B must see 5. There is a single global order of all operations, and every node agrees on it. This is a much stronger claim than "eventual consistency" or "causal consistency."

Availability means every request to a non-failing node must result in a response — not an error, not a timeout. The node must respond, even if the response is "I don't have the latest data." Crucially, availability in CAP means every node is available, not "the system as a whole responds." A system that redirects reads from a partitioned node to a primary is not CAP-available — the partitioned node itself couldn't respond.

Partition tolerance means the system continues operating despite an arbitrary number of messages being lost (or delayed) between nodes. A partition is when the network drops all messages between some subset of nodes. The system must not fail — it must continue to provide consistency or availability, or degrade gracefully.

You don't choose whether to tolerate partitions. You choose what to give up when one happens.

The proof, in one paragraph

Assume the network partitions: node A can't reach node B. A client writes x=5 to node A. Node A must either accept the write (preserving availability) or reject it (preserving consistency — it can't guarantee B will see it). If A accepts, and a client reads x from node B, B doesn't know about the write — so it either returns stale data (preserving availability, violating consistency) or refuses to answer (preserving consistency, violating availability). A single partitioned node cannot be both available and consistent. The proof is this simple because it's a constructive counterexample: here is a scenario where you must choose.

CP vs AP: the real trade

Systems that pick CP (consistent + partition-tolerant) sacrifice availability during a partition. The canonical example is a distributed lock service like etcd or ZooKeeper using Raft: if a node can't reach a quorum, it refuses reads and writes rather than serve potentially stale data. The system is unavailable, but the data it serves is always correct.

Systems that pick AP (available + partition-tolerant) sacrifice consistency during a partition. Dynamo, Cassandra (tunable, but default AP), and most CDNs accept that different nodes will return different values during a partition and resolve conflicts later — via last-write-wins, CRDTs, or application-level merge.

CA (consistent + available) is the "pick two" trap. A CA system is one that works perfectly when there's no partition. That's just a non-distributed system. Single-node databases are CA. Once you distribute across a network, partitions are physical reality — network cables get cut, switches fail, GC pauses look like partitions. A distributed CA system doesn't exist.

your call You're building a payment ledger. During a network partition, each shard can still receive writes. After the partition heals, you discover two shards both accepted a transfer from the same account at the same time, overdrawing it. Was this system CP or AP? And which choice should it have made?

The system was AP — it accepted writes during the partition. A payment ledger should almost certainly be CP: refuse writes during a partition and reconcile afterward from a single source of truth, rather than accept conflicting writes that put an account into an invalid state. The cost of unavailability (declined transactions for a few seconds) is lower than the cost of inconsistency (money created or destroyed).

Why the theorem is both essential and incomplete

CAP tells you what's impossible — you cannot have all three during a partition. It doesn't tell you what to do when there's no partition, which is most of the time. Real systems don't pick one label and wear it forever. They make the trade per-operation: most reads can be eventually consistent; a balance check during checkout should be strongly consistent. And during normal operation — no partition — you can have both consistency and availability, and you should.

The theorem also doesn't distinguish degrees. Consistency isn't binary (linearizability vs. nothing) — there's causal consistency, sequential consistency, read-your-writes. Availability isn't binary — a system that responds in 50 ms is differently available from one that responds in 10 seconds. CAP draws a sharp line because the proof requires it; engineering lives in the gray area around the line.

watch out When someone says "this database is CP," they're summarizing a design choice, not stating a mathematical fact. Most "CP" databases are actually CP for writes and eventually consistent for reads by default. And nearly every real system is actually partition-tolerant — the question is what it sacrifices during one. Ask: "during a partition, what does this system give up first?" The answer is more useful than a two-letter label.
