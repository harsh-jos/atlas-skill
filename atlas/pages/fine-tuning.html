Fine-Tuning vs RAG vs Prompting

Three ways to make a model "know" your stuff — and when each is the wrong one.

You have an LLM. You need it to handle your company's specific domain — your products, your policies, your tone. There are three ways to get there, and the wrong choice wastes months of effort or tens of thousands in inference costs. The right progression is almost always: prompt engineering → RAG → fine-tuning, in that order. Skip a step and you're solving a problem that didn't need to exist.

Prompt engineering teaches the model what you want right now. RAG gives it access to what you know. Fine-tuning bakes behavior into the weights. Each is the right answer to a different question.

Prompt engineering: the first lever

Before you modify the model in any way, modify what you tell it. A well-crafted system prompt with domain context, output format specifications, and behavioral constraints solves a surprising fraction of production needs. The cost is zero beyond inference tokens. The iteration cycle is seconds. If prompt engineering doesn't achieve your quality bar, you now have a measured gap — "the model gets format X right 80% of the time with prompting alone" — which tells you exactly what fine-tuning needs to fix.

The mistake teams make: jumping to fine-tuning because "prompt engineering didn't work" after two attempts. Prompt engineering is a discipline, not a first guess. It involves systematic variation, A/B testing, and measuring output quality quantitatively. Treat it like code: version your prompts, review changes, and test against a held-out eval set.

RAG: the knowledge lever

When the model needs access to information that changes faster than training cycles — product catalogs, documentation, customer data — prompt engineering alone breaks. The knowledge doesn't fit in the system prompt (too large) or changes too frequently (the prompt is static). RAG solves this by retrieving relevant documents at query time and injecting them into the context.

RAG is the right choice when: the knowledge changes (daily/weekly), citations are required (the user needs to know where the answer came from), or the knowledge is too large to fit in a prompt. RAG is the wrong choice when: the behavior you want is a style, tone, or reasoning pattern (bake it into the weights via fine-tuning) rather than factual knowledge.

Fine-tuning: the behavior lever

Fine-tuning is for when the model's behavior needs to change, not its knowledge. It teaches the model a consistent output format, a specific tone, or a reasoning pattern that prompt engineering can describe but not reliably enforce across diverse inputs.

In 2026, the standard approach is parameter-efficient fine-tuning: freeze most of the model's weights, inject small trainable matrices (LoRA — Low-Rank Adaptation), and train only those. A LoRA adapter adds ~0.1–3% to the parameter count and achieves 90–95% of full fine-tuning quality. QLoRA goes further: load the base model in 4-bit quantized precision, train adapters in FP16. A 70B model can be fine-tuned on a single H100 GPU in a few hours for under $10.

The fine-tuning decision framework
Problem: model outputs wrong format / tone / reasoning
  1. Can you fix it with a better prompt? → Do that.
  2. Can you fix it with few-shot examples in the prompt? → Do that.
  3. Do you have 500+ examples of the desired behavior? → Fine-tune (QLoRA).
  4. Do you NOT have 500 examples? → Generate them with a larger model first,
     filter for quality, then fine-tune.
predict You fine-tune a model on 5,000 examples of your support team's responses. The model nails the tone. But when a customer asks about the new return policy (changed last week), the model gives the old answer confidently. What's the failure, and what's the architectural fix?

Knowledge staleness. Fine-tuning bakes knowledge into the weights. The model learned the old return policy during fine-tuning and has no way to know it changed. The fix: hybrid architecture. Fine-tune for tone and format (the stable behavior). Use RAG for policy documents (the volatile knowledge). The fine-tuned model generates the response in the right tone; RAG supplies the actual policy text. This is the standard production pattern in 2026: fine-tune the behavior, retrieve the knowledge. Trying to make fine-tuning handle both is the most common failure mode of the approach.

the real cost of fine-tuning It's not the training — QLoRA on an 8B model costs ~$2 and takes 45 minutes. It's the lifecycle: you now own a model. When the base model updates (Llama 3.1 → 3.2), you redo the fine-tune. When your training data changes (new product categories), you redo the fine-tune. When you discover a failure mode, you collect examples and redo the fine-tune. The compute cost is trivial; the data curation, evaluation, and retraining cadence are the real, ongoing investment. Teams that treat fine-tuning as a one-time project are surprised by the maintenance burden. Teams that treat it as a training pipeline with scheduled retraining cycles are not.
