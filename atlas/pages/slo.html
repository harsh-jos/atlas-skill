SLIs, SLOs & Error Budgets

Turning "be reliable" into a number you can spend — and the framework that ends the war between product and SRE.

Somewhere in your organization, an engineer is being asked to make the system "more reliable." The engineer asks "how reliable?" and gets back "very reliable." This is the pre-SRE world — reliability as a vibe, argued about in meetings, traded against features in a negotiation where neither side has numbers.

The SRE framework (from Google's Site Reliability Engineering book, 2016, and now industry standard) replaces the vibe with three concepts that fit on an index card:

  • SLI (Service Level Indicator): a measured number. "The proportion of requests that returned a successful response within 300ms, measured over a rolling 28-day window."
  • SLO (Service Level Objective): a target for that number. "99.9% of requests succeed within 300ms." This is negotiated between the service owners and the business.
  • Error Budget: the gap between the SLO and 100%. If your SLO is 99.9%, your error budget is 0.1% of requests — roughly 43 minutes of downtime per month. When the error budget is positive, you can ship features. When it's exhausted, you freeze features and invest in reliability.
An error budget turns reliability into a resource you can spend, not an absolute you can only fail to meet.

The error budget is the point

The SLI and SLO exist to produce the error budget. The error budget exists to resolve the fundamental tension between development velocity and reliability. Before error budgets, the conversation was: "we want to ship faster" vs "we want fewer incidents." Both are reasonable, neither is measurable, and the person with more organizational power wins.

With an error budget, the conversation becomes: "we have 22 minutes of budget remaining this month. This feature has a risk profile that might consume 5 minutes. We can ship it." Or: "we've exhausted our error budget for the quarter. All feature work is paused until we've invested enough in reliability to bring the SLI back within SLO." The decision is data-driven and the framework is agreed upon in advance. No one is the bad guy — the numbers decide.

What makes a good SLI

Not everything that can be measured should be an SLI. A good SLI is:

  • User-visible. CPU utilization is not an SLI; it's an internal metric. Users don't care about your CPU; they care about latency and errors. Track what the user experiences. (Internal SLIs for dependencies are fine, but start with user-facing.)
  • High-cardinality enough to matter. "99.9% of all requests succeed" can mask a problem: the 0.1% of failures might all be in the payment path. Break SLIs down by critical user journeys — checkout, login, search — not just aggregate request counts.
  • Measurable from the client side where possible. Server-side metrics miss DNS failures, CDN issues, and client network problems. A load balancer reporting 100% success while your users see a blank page is worse than no metric at all.

The standard starting set — the "golden signals" from the SRE book:

  1. Latency: How long does it take to serve a request? Distinguish between successful and error responses (a fast 500 is still an error).
  2. Traffic: How much demand is the system under? Requests per second, segmented by type.
  3. Errors: What fraction of requests fail? Explicit failures (500s), implicit failures (200 OK with wrong content), and degraded responses (served from cache when the primary is down).
  4. Saturation: How full is the system? Queue depth, thread pool utilization, connection count. The leading indicator of future latency and errors.
predict Your SLO is 99.9% availability. Last month you had 99.95% — well within budget. This month, a deployment at 3am causes a 45-minute outage. It's the 15th of the month. What does the error budget say, and what happens to your next planned feature deploy?

At 99.9% SLO, your monthly error budget is about 43 minutes (0.1% × 30 days × 24 × 60). The 45-minute outage consumed the entire monthly budget plus 2 minutes. The error budget is now negative. Per the framework the team agreed to: feature freezes are in effect until the budget recovers — either by the 28-day rolling window expiring the outage, or by enough incident-free time accumulating to bring the window's error rate back under 0.1%. The next feature deploy waits. This isn't punishment — it's the contract. The business agreed that 99.9% was the right number, and the error budget mechanism enforces it without requiring a VP to intervene.

Choosing the SLO number

The most common mistake is setting SLOs too high. 99.99% ("four nines") sounds reasonable but permits 4 minutes of downtime per month. That's not a target; that's a budget for one incident investigation. The practical rule:

  • Don't set an SLO higher than your dependencies can support. If your cloud provider's SLA is 99.95%, your SLO can't be 99.99% — you'll exhaust your error budget from your dependencies' outages alone, before your own code has a chance to fail.
  • Start loose, tighten later. Set the initial SLO based on current measured performance. If you're currently at 99.5%, set the SLO at 99.5% and invest in reliability until you're comfortably above it, then tighten to 99.9%. An SLO you're already violating on day one teaches the team that SLOs don't matter.
  • Not every service needs the same SLO. The payment service might be 99.99%. The recommendation engine might be 99%. The internal dashboard might be 99% with no error budget tracking at all. Reliability is a product decision, not a moral imperative.
the trap Error budgets work when the organization trusts the framework. If a VP overrides the freeze because "this feature is too important," the framework loses all credibility and you're back to vibes. The hardest part of implementing SLOs is not the measurement — it's the organizational discipline to honor the error budget when it says no. If your company isn't ready for that, don't adopt SLOs. A framework that's routinely ignored is worse than no framework at all.
